KEY PROJECTS

Here, we have talked about some of the major PPLV research projects which the UCL team had worked on and their impact on the world:

Resource Reasoning

This is a four-year project starting from 01 March 2012 to 30 June 2016. Led by Professor D from University College London, it is categorised under fundamentals of computing and computer systems & architectures.

It’s main idea is about resource. Resource has always been a crucial aspect to be considered for all computer programmes and systems. Suitable and efficient usages of recourses such as memory, files, processor time must be ensured for healthy and sustainable working of systems. Thus it is natural for programmers to consider thoroughly about how the system deals with and allocates resource. The research team believes that there can be a new kind of mathematical theory, that is called resource reasoning, which connects closely with engineers' informal thinking about resources.

The research team believes that resource should be considered not only in pre-formal system design, but the formal analysis and verification of programs as well now and in future. Undoubtedly, it is difficult to develop a mature theory (or theories) that both engages with their informal hunches, and generates tractable, reliable analysis and verification techniques for many problem. The research aimed new theoretical facts and new prototypes which which could help to eliminate frequent crash, mishandling of data and to enhance reliability.

The team has collaborated with researchers from other institutions to release a number of valuable paper regarding this topic:

  • “Sub-Classical Boolean Bunched Logics and the Meaning of Par” By James Brotherston and Jules Villard
  • “A decision procedure for satisfiability in separation logic with inductive predicates (2014)” By James Brotherston, Carsten Fuhs, Juan A. Navarro Perez and Nikos Gorogianni
  • “Sub-Classical Boolean Bunched Logics and the Meaning of Par” By Gabrielle Anderson and David Pym
  • “A calculus and logic of bunched resources and processes” By Gabrielle Anderson and David Pym

The results developed in this project are used for new projects in diverse areas and are applied in security policies in human-centred organizations. The researchers involved in this projects are now working for big companies like Amazon, Facebook and Google. They might fuse these ideas to their R&D to impact more areas.

Find out more from their official website: http://www.resourcereasoning.com/

Compositional Security Analysis for Binaries

This project started on 26 November 2013 and ended on 25 November 2016. The research team led by Professor B Cook consisted researchers from University College London(UCL).

This project focus on compositional analysis for binary code. Binaries are commonly studied by various groups such as the intelligence community, military organisations and security engineers. Their vulnerabilities are widely discussed. Binaries are usually huge thus relevant analysis and verification techniques must be scalable. The scientific challenge in this research is the method to separate sophisticated interactions, prevent expensive operations, and derive procedure summaries which are solid.

The UCL research team developed basic techniques to conduct the analysis, testing their viability with a running case study: the data sanitization problem. Confidential data is sanitised when its memory is zeroed before it is deallocated, preventing an attacker retrieving the sensitive information.

Along their studies, the UCL team had released a series of academic papers regarding the specific areas under this topic:

  • “Reasoning about Nondeterminism in Programs” By Byron Cook and Eric Koskinen.
  • “Faster Temporal Reasoning for infinite-state programs (2014)” By Byron Cook, Heidy Khlaaf, Nir Piterman
  • “Tools and Algorithms for the Construction and Analysis of Systems (2014)” By Hong-Yi Chen, Byron Cook, Carsten Fuhs, Kaustubh Nimkar, Peter O’Hearn
  • “Computer Aided Verification (2013)”in the book”Better Termination Proving through Cooperation” by Marc Brockschmidt, Byron Cook, Carsten Fuhs

This topic appears highly interesting to many researchers. Other universities in UK like University of Kent, Imperial College London, and Queen Mary, University of London were also working on this aspect and have released publications which influence and push the development of this research field. Now, this method of analysis has been adapted and used in industries.

Program Verification Techniques for Understanding Security Properties of Software

This is a project started from October, 2013 to April, 2017, led by Karp, B.N. and O’Hearn, P.. It is categorised as ‘Information and Communication Technology’.

The project collaborated with industries like Google and Microsoft, aiming to develop an automatic program verification method that allows security engineers to get familiar with softwares that were not coded by themselves. The method will even point out possible flaws in the program and advise engineers on where to fix or improve by ‘discover logical descriptions of execution environments (preconditons, protocols, invariants, etc.) that pinpoint the assumptions necessary for code safety or those that trigger violations’.

Papers published in collaboration with Google, Mozilla Research, Stanford University and UCSD are as follows:

  • http://gtr.rcuk.ac.uk/projects?ref=EP%2FK032542%2F1

The research team has developed:

  • ‘Confinement with Origin Web Labels (COWL)’, which could be deployed in mainstream web browsers and be used by the public; the activity to standardise COWL is ongoing.
  • G8, a run-time policy system for the popular Node.js JavaScript development framework for server-side applications. By enforcing security invariants at runtime, G8 blocks unsafe operations and unauthorised disclosures of sensitive information that would otherwise have resulted from programmer errors.

Partial order semantics for concurrent program verification

This is a project started from February, 2013 to February, 2017, led by Alglave, J.. It is categorised as ‘Information and Communication Technology’.

Current programming method is error-prone when dealing with multiprocessor systems. One PostgreSQL bug is caused by this issue and is hard to reproduce or observe. Bugs like this can hardly be detected in tests, therefore the research team wanted to invent an automated techniques that analyse the code without executing it with partial order semanics, freeing programmers from writing proofs for their code.

Papers published in collaboration with IBM and Monoidics Ltd are as follows:

http://gtr.rcuk.ac.uk/projects?ref=EP%2FK039431%2F1

The researcher was able to apply his work to the prototype of a future Nvidia chip.
